So Long, T-RA!

 

This is the last issue of the IEEE Transactions on Robotics and Automation (T-RA). In July, the first issue of the new IEEE Transactions on Automation Science and Engineering (T-ASE) will appear. Next August, the first IEEE Transactions on Robotics (T-RO) will be published as Issue 4 of Volume 20 in the T-RA series.

 

The current status of the two T-RA sibling journals appears to be highly promising. Under the Editorship of Professor Peter Luh, T-ASE received 60 submissions since the call for papers issued in June 2003 and just about the same number in the first four months of 2004. In turn, T-RO received 90 submissions in the last three months of 2003, while this year 120 new submissions have been received as of the beginning of May. The goal of becoming the two flagship journals respectively in Automation and in Robotics has received indeed a bright start.

 

In this last editorial, however, I think it is appropriate to look back at the past 20 years of T-RA history. The following list of facts and figures may provide a more concrete sense of the impressive achievements.

 

The quarterly IEEE Journal of Robotics and Automation (J-RA) started in March 1985, under the lead of the Founding Editor George Bekey, with four Associate Editors and 13 Technical Editors. The first 65-page issue contained a survey, four regular papers, a communication, and a book review. In the first year, J-RA published 215 pages. The journal became bimonthly in 1987 and in 1989, with the Robotics and Automation Council becoming an IEEE Society, it changed its name to the current IEEE Transactions on Robotics and Automation, together with receiving a new Editor, Professor Russell Taylor. In that year, T-RA published 825 pages. From there, the Transactions continued to grow constantly under the lead of Professors Dick Volz (from August 1994 to June 1999), Peter Luh (from August 1999 to August 2003), and myself for the last few months. During the last few years, the page count stayed consistently at over 1000 pages per year.

 

In 113 issues, T-RA published about 16200 pages with 1066 Regular Papers, 532 Short Papers (variously denoted over the years), 53 comments, replies, errata, and reports (under the current Communication Item category), 28 Book Reviews, 9 Survey Papers (notably for the field, most of these appeared during the first years), and 24 Editorials (included those launching the 16 Special Issues or Sections of the journal).

 

Indeed, the number of submitted manuscripts in the broad sense has been much higher. In 1985, there were 87 submissions (and no data base!). Unfortunately, I have available the full data only from 1991, when 301 submissions were received. Between then and September 30, 2003, when submission to T-RA has been closed, a total of 4268 manuscripts were received.

 

This huge number of scientific works was evaluated by Reviewers (typically three for each paper) and members of the Editorial Board. Since the beginning, the journal has had the same four-tier reviewing system (the Reviewers and the currently named Associate Editors, Editors, and Editor-in-Chief), with the purpose of guaranteeing a more uniform balance and fair judgment of research themes. Since the first published listing in 1992, about 8000 Reviewers were involved (many of them are repeatedly listed over the years, while a large majority reviewed multiple papers in a single year). Their work has been coordinated by more than 120 Associate Editors, selected from among the best scientists in the field and with the additional feature of offering their voluntary work to our community, and supervised by 19 Editors. I take this opportunity to collectively thank them all again.

 

The international flavor of our Transactions is also attested to by the number of country affiliations of corresponding authors: 30 countries were involved in the submissions of year 2003.

 

Other major milestones included the move in February 1999 to OPeRA, the IEEE online access to Transactions papers that later evolved into IEEE Xplore, and the passage to electronic submission and to a web-based paper review system, started in August 1999. Currently, all submitted papers are in electronic form, and all communications are by email.

 

Beyond counting all the above numbers, quality has been always the Transactions’ primary reference index. We are indeed proud of the T-RA impact factor (from ISI Journal Citation Report), ranked first in the Automation and Robotics area in the last five years. An average acceptance rate of just below 30% contributed to this success. The continuous flow of over 400 submissions for the third year in a row is another index of perceived quality.

 

Another relevant aspect for perspective authors is timeliness. The Editorial Board worked hard to reduce as much as possible the submission-to-decision time. Here, my memory helps. When I started as Associate Editor in 1994, I remember the Board striving to reduce the number of papers in review for more than nine months. In 1999, we attacked the papers with 6-9 months reviewing times. In the last year, we ended up with efforts in reducing the average time within the 3-6 months category. The submission-to-decision average time is currently 149 days. Electronic handling of submission helped a lot, but the commitment of people, which cannot be automated, should never be underestimated.

 

A final parameter worth attention is the submission-to-publication time, which includes also the time needed for final revision by the authors of accepted papers, the processing by IEEE Transactions and Journals Department (3 weeks) and, last but not least, the constraint on yearly page budget. Here, we are still over the one-year target, but working jointly with all partners involved for a rapid improvement.

 

No scientific journal may reach its primary scope and satisfy the readership without high quality submissions by the authors. It is quite hard to single out the most relevant and innovative papers originally published by the T-RA –and forgive me if I will not undertake this exercise! However these certainly encompass most of the areas listed in our cover page. For searching research subjects treated over the years, viewing and downloading T-RA published papers (all are on-line, including those which appeared from 1985 in the legacy Journal), as well as performing your own statistics, I invite you to use the increasingly powerful IEEE Xplore system, available to IEEE RAS members and through subscribing libraries and institutions (see http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/xpl/RecentIssue.jsp?puNumber=70).

 

For upcoming news and future directions, please check Peter Luh’s T-ASE editorial in July and in my next forward-looking editorial in the T-RO August issue.

 

Now it is finally time to say…Arrivederci, T-RA!
