Combinatorial algorithms

We shall work in three important general areas of algorithm design, namely on-line/dynamic algorithms, optimisation/approximation algorithms and string algorithms. The need for dynamic and on-line algorithms arises in problems which vary over time and in which large streams of requests (updates, queries) must be processed rapidly. We plan to tackle the new challenges of this area, such as the design of dynamic algorithms for max flow and reachability problems, the average case analysis of dynamic algorithms, and lower bounds for dynamic graphs. We shall also develop on-line algorithms for network problems, such as on-line routing and call admission, as well as scheduling and planning problems. Furthermore, we shall consider specific logistics problems relevant in our industrial collaborations. We shall also explore the use of randomisation and parallelism to obtain more competitive solutions.

In some hard optimisation problems that must be solved in practice, one often looks for approximate solutions with a small distance from optimality. The branch-and-cut technique, which combines polyhedral combinatorics with efficient separation algorithms, appears to be the most promising recent line of attack in this important area (many practical instances of the traveling salesman problem of a few hundred nodes can be solved to optimality, and instances with a few thousand nodes to within, say, 1% of optimality in reasonable time). We want to apply this novel technique to combinatorial optimisation problems that play an important role in applications. In particular, we plan to investigate the determination of maximum planar subgraphs under side conditions, relevant in automated graph drawing, and the maximum cut problem, relevant in circuit layout design and statistical physics. We shall also apply other approximation techniques to other difficult optimisation problems, such as graph bisection, which have applications in high-performance computer networks, computer-integrated manufacturing environments, and graph drawing. Furthermore, we shall study the class APX of NP-complete optimisation problems that allow approximation algorithms within a constant ratio of optimality, and search for efficient parallel approximation algorithms for P-complete problems. An important technique that we shall pursue is the use of random walks on Markov chains defined by combinatorial problems. This enables approximate counting and almost uniform generation, and yields approximate randomised solutions to several important problems.

In many applications, such as lexicography and computational biology, there is an urgent need for new techniques and data structures for the retrieval of textual information from huge data bases. We shall address basic problems common to many applications, such as pattern-matching and string-alignment: searching a large set of strings to find all occurrences of substrings that are ``similar'' to a given pattern, and determining similarities among strings under a distance measure which may reflect mutations, experimental error or ``noise''. We analyse periodicity structures in strings and trees (an essential aspect for efficient searching algorithms), and exploit the powerful technique of hash functions for string processing. Applications will be made to computational biology, where we shall develop algorithms for protein identification through mass fingerprinting and approximate algorithms for the construction of phylogenetic trees. Some of this work will be implemented in a library of efficient string algorithms and exploited for computational biochemistry.
