Randomness and analysis of algorithms

The efficiency of many algorithms and data structures relies on the exploitation of properties of combinatorial structures known to occur with a high probability. Our objective is to develop systematic methods for predicting the average-case or probabilistic behaviour of complex combinatorial objects, such as those arising in the ALCOM-IT project. First, we shall study the critical parameters of random combinatorial structures which determine performance. Random graphs, dynamic graphs, and circuits are directly relevant to models of computation as well as to the analysis of optimisation algorithms and distributed systems. Random trees and random strings will be applied to problems in stringology, distributed computing, general data structures for searching, packet routing, on-line algorithms, etc. Research on random logical formulae provides useful insight for hard combinatorial problems. Second, we shall systematise the theory of `decomposable' models of randomness, with the goal of characterising classes of problems that are decidable. This is closely intertwined with computer algebra that provides the proper implementation framework. We shall develop the LUO system by redesigning it within Maple, structuring it into modules that can be used separately. The goal is to develop a coherent collection of algorithms and libraries, for simulating complex combinatorial models, for explicitly solving recurrences, and for asymptotic analysis. A major emphasis will be on robust methods with guaranteed correctness, based on mixed numeric-symbolic algorithms for efficiency. The programs will then be usable for the exact analysis of large models (e.g., large finite state models) providing precise estimates of implied constants and asymptotic convergence rates.
