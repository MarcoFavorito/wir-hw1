Editorial

 

With this October issue, the transition from IEEE Transactions on Robotics and Automation (T-RA) to the two new journals of the IEEE Robotics and Automation Society (RA-S) starts coming into action. Professor Peter Luh has just ended his term as Editor-in-Chief of T-RA and moved to the full-time lead of the new sibling, the IEEE Transactions on Automation Science and Engineering (T-ASE). I am honored to take over his responsibilities for T-RA in the delicate but exciting process of transformation towards the IEEE Transactions on Robotics (T-RO). The first issues of the two Transactions will appear almost simultaneously in the middle of 2004, T-ASE in July and T-RO in August.

 

In the Editorial which appeared in last June’s issue, Peter Luh overviewed the impressive achievements of T-RA during the last few years. T-RA is headed toward a number of submissions of over 400 papers for the third year in a row, with submission-to-decision review times of 150 days on the average, an acceptance rate just below 30%, and the highest impact factor in the field. As readers, authors, and members of the Editorial Board, we should thank Peter for his successful efforts and dedication to quality and timeliness. When IEEE voted for the splitting of T-RA, under the auspices of RA-S past and present Presidents Steve Hsia and Paolo Dario, the healthy operation of the current T-RA certainly played an important role, together with the professional and enthusiastic work of the Vice President for Publications, Professor Richard Volz, and the Steering and Advisory Committees promoting T-ASE, chaired by Professor Ken Goldberg.

 

The scope of T-ASE and its declared goal of becoming the flagship journal in Automation have already been presented in the June editorial. Complementary to this, bringing to fruition the high expectations that our community has for the new Transactions on Robotics will be my primary goal. T-RO shall publish scientific papers on all aspects involving robots and intelligent machines and systems, from fundamental theory of interdisciplinary nature to innovative applications for industry, for remote and unstructured environments, and for human assistance and service. In addition, our efforts will be devoted to address issues of Robotics science “and beyond,” exciting borderline or strategic subjects that may become essential in the long run. In short, T-RO will not be just a renaming of T-RA. It will expand upon the excellent reputation of that journal and carry the Society’s coverage of robotics to new levels.

 

Let me provide some additional operational information for this transition period.

 

Under my supervision, the Editorial Board of T-RA will complete the reviews of all papers that have been submitted to T-RA up to the end of September 2003. T-RA will continue publishing accepted papers until its last issue, which will appear in June 2004. Please direct questions about the review status or the publication schedule of T-RA papers to me at deluca@dis.uniroma1.it or to my Editorial Assistant, Ms. Daniela Lo Tenero, at ieeetro@dis.uniroma1.it. Due to the current publication backlog, it is likely that a number of accepted papers will not find a place within the few last issues of T-RA. In strict cooperation with the Editor-in-Chief of T-ASE, the authors of these papers will be contacted after acceptance and offered the opportunity to choose whether their papers will be transfered to T-RO or to T-ASE for publication. Authors wishing to resubmit a revised version of their papers originally reviewed for T-RA will have a similar choice.

 

On October 1, 2003, the new T-RO begins accepting submissions, aligning itself to the current situation of T-ASE, whose call for papers started in June. Perspective authors in the field of Robotics and Automation will thus have a choice for the venue of their papers, depending on the actual contributions of the work. Indeed, there will still be overlapping themes that are within the scope of both Transactions. Detailed submission information can be found by tracing the web sites www.ieee.org/t-ro or www.ieee.org/t-ase. If you need further information, do not hesitate to contact either one of the two Editors-in-Chief of T-RO or T-ASE. Peter and I will continue to strictly cooperate on this and other issues in order to guarantee a smooth transition.

 

The legacy of T-RO which will be inherited from T-RA is evidenced by several formal and substantial facts. T-RO will have the same Editorial Board of T-RA, as it stands at the end of September 2003. The composition of the Board will then evolve gradually to its Robotics focus. T-RO shall continue with the volume/issue numbering and bimonthly schedule of T-RA, keeping also the same cover appearance. Since the percentage of robotics-related papers that have appeared in T-RA is around 75% of the total, at steady state T-RO will have enough space to further expand its coverage, both in the traditional areas and of new or emerging topics of interest to Robotics.

 

Finally, I would like to express my sincere gratitude to everybody who helped me during my editorial activity for the Transactions, which I begun in 1994 as an Associate Editor and then in 1998 as one of the Editors. In particular, without the support and the international views of my previous two Editors-in-Chief, Professor Richard Volz and Professor Peter Luh, I would not be sharing my thoughts here with you now.

 

The king is dead, long live the kings! T-RA is going to disappear, but both T-RO and T-ASE are looking forward to receiving fine contributions by authors from all over the world and having reviewers who devote their expertise to further improve quality, with the goal of publishing papers that disseminate science and technical advances for a better life.

 

 

Alessandro De Luca, Editor-in-Chief
