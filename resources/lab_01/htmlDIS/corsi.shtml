Programma

FIRST PART (April 11-15, 2005)

Constraint satisfaction problems constitute a broad class of algorithmic problems that arise naturally in several different areas of computer science and artificial intelligence. The aim of this course is to present an overview of results in constraint satisfaction with emphasis on the computational complexity of constraint satisfaction problems and on the connections of this area of research with logic, universal algebra, and database theory.

Topics covered include:

(1) Examples and formal definition of constraint satisfaction problems (CSP); CSP and the homomorphism problem.

(2) CSP and database theory: connections with the conjunctive query evaluation problem and the conjunctive query containment problem,

(3) Complexity of CSP: Schaefer's dichotomy theory for the complexity of Boolean non-uniform CSP; the Feder-Vardi dichotomy conjecture for the complexity of non-uniform CSP.

(4) The pursuit of tractable cases of CSP: closure properties of constraints and connections with universal algebra; Datalog, pebble games and consistency properties of CSP; bounded treewidth and CSP;

Algorithms for Managing Massive Data Streams (Maggio-Giugno 2004)
Docente
 S. Muthu Muthukrishnan, Rutgers University, NJ
Info

Stefano Leonardi
Dove

Dipartimento di Informatica e Sistemistica, Via Salaria 113, II piano - Aula C3
Programma

In emerging applications, data comes in very fast and in large volume. So, often we need algorithms that summarize the data quickly in small space, and use that for approximate calculations later. The quintessential application is in monitoring IP network traffic for security, provisioning, etc. but other applications include monitoring text streams like email or SMS, geometric data from sensor data streams, etc. In the past few years there has been tremendous developments in design and analysis of algorithms to handle data streams, principles for building Data Stream Management Systems akin to Data Base Management Systems, and application studies in IP traffic analysis with operational systems. In this course, I will cover the algorithmic foundations of the data streams area in detail. Applications to databases and IP traffic analyses will be discussed. Throughout, I will present exercises and open problems for researchers.

Contents:

1. Overview of Data Streams: Models and Applications.
2. Data Stream Algorithms for summaries: quantiles, heavy hitters, deltoids, wavelets and histograms.
3. Data Stream Algorithms for clustering.
4. Data stream algorithms for text applications.
5. Overview of applications.

Part I: The structure of the Internet and the Web graph
Part 2: Game theory, computational issues.
Prof. E. Koutsoupias

In the first part, we will discuss power-law models that try to capture the structure of the Internet and the Web graphs. In the second part, we will discuss the concepts and some computational issues for Games and Nash equilibria.

The price of anarchy
Prof. E. Koutsoupias

We will study the price of anarchy for task allocation (the Koutsoupias-Papadimitriou model) and selfish routing (the Roughgarden-Tardos model). We will also discuss non-atomic and atomic congestion games.

Thursday, October 23rd - 14:00 - 17:00
Mechanism design and multicast cost sharing

We will discuss the concept of mechanism design and focus on VCG mechanisms. We will then study algorithmic issues of mechanism design (the Nissan-Ronen problem). We will also study algorithmic issues of cost sharing mechanisms for multicast transmission.

Monday, November 3rd, 14:00 - 17:00
Primal-dual methods for the design of approximation algorithms
Prof. Jochen Koenemann

We will discuss primal-dual methods as a tool of design and analysis of polynomial time algorithms to approximate NP-hard combinatorial problems. We will present algorithms for classical problems such as vertex cover and set cover, Goemans and Williamson's algorithm for steiner tree and Jain and Vazirani's algorithm for facility location.

Wendsday, November 5th, 14:00 - 17:00
Cost sharing mechanisms for network design problems I
Prof. Stefano Leonardi

We will present cost sharing mechanisms for network design problems that are based on primal-dual methods. The algorithms are cross-monotonic, competitive and guarantee to recover a share of the cost of the solution. We will present Jain and Vazirani's result on steiner tree and Pal and Tardos's result on facility location games.

Friday, November 7th, 14:00 - 17:00
Cost sharing mechanisms for network design problems II
Prof. Stefano Leonardi

We will further extend the study of cross monotonic mechanisms. We will present Pal and Tardos' mechanism for single-sink rent-or-buy problems, and present Gupta, Kumar and Roughgarden's algorithm.

Human-Computer Interaction - post-millennial models (Maggio 2003)
Docente
 Professor Alan Dix
Computing Department - Lancaster University, UK

Alan Dix has worked in human-computer interaction research since the mid 1980's and he is author of one of the main textbooks in the area. He has also been a founder director of two Internet startup companies focused on intelligent agent technology and web community building. His nearly two hundred publications include his popular ACM SIGCHI Bulletin education column, five books, many journal articles, conference keynotes, and invited contributions to several recent handbooks in HCI; they cover topics from the application of formal techniques in interface design, through understanding the physical workplace, to methods for enhancing innovation and creativity.
Info

Tiziana Catarci
Descrizione

The aim if this course is to both give a taste of traditional methods in Human Computer Interaction, but also to ask how the theories and models developed for GUIs in the 1980s match up to ubiquitious computing and novel interfaces in the new millenium.

The course is organized in four parts, which will be nearly independent. My overall stance is both theoretical and practical in that I believe that it is deep knowledge that gives the ability to address real issues in innovate ways. Furthermore I hope that it will enable you to see potential research directions within the field.

Basics of HCI and cognition - I will assume no previous knowledge and so start the course with a very brief 'lightening' overview of HCI as a design discipline and as a research field. However, if you have some previous knowledge of HCI I hope this will still be of interest. In this part I will also cover some issues in visual perception and about temporal issues of the human and interaction. This is largely done as an example of the way deeper psychological knowledge can inform our understanding.

Human context - This strand is focused on ways in which we can understand human interactions taking into account the factors human and physical outside of the computer system itself. As well as traditional techniques such as task analysis and scenarios, we will look at the ways in which task analysis can be extended to include the ways in which humans interact with the physical environment. Many ubiquitous computing applications are very different from traditional ones and we will also consider how models of human activity are challenged by these applications.

System definition - This strand will look at more formal ways of describing the way interactive computer systems behave. Again this will include both 'traditional' specification techniques and also a consideration of issues such as continuous devices prompted by novel interfaces. Also the issue of interaction through physical devices will be important and we'll talk about the way in which physical controls map on to the logical behaviour of devices. As one example of the use of formal methods I will refer to a previous La Sapienza PhD thesis!

At the edge - The final part of the course will deal with some issues that are at the edge, but likely to become central, in computer systems design. As well as being useful and usable, systems must be used by people, and for this they must want to use them. We will therefore look at how to design engaging experiences and also how to design systems that firm in with the users own values. As part of this final part, we will also look at general issues of innovation and creative research - how to produce new ideas that are innovate and yet actually solve problems! This is important for all research, problem solving and novel product development in industry.Some of the material will be based on new chapters from the 3rd edition of Human-Computer Interaction (Dix, Finlay, Abowd and Beale) being published later this year. Pre-prints of draft chapters will be available for these parts.

Cognitive Robotics (Maggio 2002)
Docenti
 Yves Lesperance (York University, Toronto, Canada) Giuseppe De Giacomo, Daniele Nardi, Fiora Pirri
Info

Giuseppe De Giacomo
Sito del corso
Descrizione

Cognitive robotics is the study of the knowledge representation and reasoning problems faced by an autonomous agent in a dynamic and incompletely known world. It can also be thought of as an attempt to make cognition more relevant to robotic control. The course provides an overview of some current research in this area, including a more in-depth look at the Situation Calculus-based approach.

The course is organized in 9 lectures of 2 hours each plus 2 hours of students seminars.

The evaluation of the students taking the course for credit will be based on a seminar given by the student on a research topic selected in consultation with the instructors.
