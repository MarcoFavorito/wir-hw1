Robotic Exploration of Unknown Environments

The Sensor-based Random Tree (SRT) Method

 

The SRT method is a randomized technique that we have developed for exploring unknown environments using mobile robots equipped with simple sensors (ultrasonic and/or infrared rangefinders). The basic tool is the randomized generation of the next robot configuration "close to the border" of the safe region as perceived at the current configuration. To this end, we have adopted (and adapted) LaValle's Rapidly-exploring Random Tree (RRT) concept.

Depending on the perception mode (conservative or confident) we obtain two instances of the basic method, respectively called SRT-Ball and SRT-Star. The first estimates the local safe region as a disk, while the second uses a (proper) star-shaped set accounting for the directionality of the sensors.

Documents

The SRT method has been designed and developed by: G. Oriolo, M. Vendittelli, L. Freda, G. Troso.

Simulations

Realized within the Move3D (a powerful motion planning software environment realized at LAAS-CNRS) under the assumption of perfect sensing and localization, these simulation clips highlight the essential exploratory qualities of SRT-Ball and SRT-Star.

Simulation 1: SRT-Ball (RM clip, AVI clip) vs. SRT-Star (RM clip, AVI clip). Note how SRT-Star has a more pronounced depth-first attitude with respect to SRT-Ball, whose tree is more expanded in width.

Simulation 2: SRT-Ball (RM clip, AVI clip) vs. SRT-Star (RM clip, AVI clip). The superiority of SRT-Star in negotiating narrow passages is clear.
