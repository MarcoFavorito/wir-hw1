Research at the Nakamura-Okada Lab at the University of Tokyo, Japan ( 27 Apr 2004 - ... )

 

The aim of the research is to design a walking controller for the robot Theta, developed at the Nakmura laboratory. It is a humanoid robot, with a total of 23 joints, each leg with 6 joints. There are several interesting characteristics about this robot:

  • Double Spherical hip joint
  • Hybrid Knee Joint: it can switch between a passive and active mode of operation, thus allowing the study of free swinging dynamic of the leg
  • Size and weight comparable to human body
  • Human-like feet

 

 

 

 

 

First Motion of Theta

( July 1st 2004 ) VIDEO

 

This motion is generated by a simple open loop controller. Basically it is a motion planner, which generates the reference trajectory for both feet in cartesian space. The joint coordinates reference is then obtained by the inverse kinematic algorithm, which runs in real time. The inverse kinematic is in closed form, thus considerably reducing the computational cost, compared to a numerical method.

Standing on two feet

( July 28th 04 ) VIDEO

 

After everything was put back together the first standing experiment has been carried out. The robot is standin on both feet, and a motion is generated such that the projection of the center of mass (COM) constantly lies in the support polygon ( area between the feet ). In this way the robot is always in static equilibrium. The motion is open-loop, so no data from the sensors is being used (force sensors at the feet, and gyros in the main body). This demonstrate the accuracy of the model parameters such as masses and COM location for each link. We are now designing a control algorithm the stabilized this motion further by using feedback from the sensors.

 

 

 

 

First Force Control experiment

( July 30th 04) VIDEO

 

This is the first time that the output of the force sensors is used. The initial positio of the left ankle roll joint is set wrong. In this configuration the foot is not in flat contact with the ground, thus causing a high reaction torque. This is measured by the 6 axis force-torque sensor, that is positioned between the last joint and the foot sole. At a certain time the feedback controller is started, and the ankle position is corrected so as to bring the foot in flat contact with the ground, thus reducing the reaction torque to zero.

 

 

 

 

Lateral COG Stabilization via Force Sensor Feedback

( July 30th 04) VIDEO

 

Using force sensor information a lateral COG equilibrium correction is carried out. The goal is to bring the projection of the COG on the ground as close as possible to the middle point between the feet. In this way the static equilibrium is improved. The controller uses the vertical reaction force values from both feet.

 

 

       

Equilibrium Control Using Force Sensors 

( Sept 27th 2004) Video

 

In this motion the force and torque information from the support foot force sensor is used to control the equilibrium. This data are used to compute the error in the ZMP location, and so the trajectory of the swing foot is modified in order to have a stable configuration at the end of the single support phase. The video shows one experiment in which the swing foot (right) is supposed to be lifted from the ground, and put back down in the same place. However, due to errors in the joints, the robot tends to fall backward. The equilibrium is then maintained by the controller, which bring the swing foot backward in order to regain equilibrium after the impact.

 

 

 

 

 

 

FIRST 4 STEPS!!!

( Oct 2nd 2004) Video

 

This is the first experiment with the whole locomotion and equilibrium controller in action. The robot is commanded to execute 4 steps ( 2 left single support and 2 right single support ). The step length is supposed to be 20cm, but this length change in each step due to the action of the equilibrium controller. The main stability problem comes from the elasticity in the knee, and the behaviour is not simmetric between left and right knee. We are currently working on improving the knee controller performance, in order to increase the overall stability.

Posture correction during walking

( Oct 12th 2004) Videos: Front  Side Perspective

 

The robot is now correcting its posture while it is walking, so that it adjusts its equilibrium and regain balance after each step. With this compensation the various errors during each steps are compensated, and a more stable walking is achieved. The videos show a 6 steps walking.
