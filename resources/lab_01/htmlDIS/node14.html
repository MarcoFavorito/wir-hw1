Parallel and distributed computing, and computational models

Recent technological achievements require advances beyond the existing computational models in order to be used effectively. We shall investigate different models and provide new fundamental results and methods of general use. Pragmatic aspects of current and future computer systems will be modelled so that realistic estimates of efficiency can be given for algorithms in these new settings. First, we shall investigate physical realisations of PRAM properties, e.g., optical technology for concurrent memory access, and consider the power of some novel models such as the PRAM with I/O, parallel quantum computers, or ``molecular'' computers. Second, we shall seek detailed models, like BSP and LogP, to represent the capabilities of existing machines and draw programmers' attention to important implementation aspects. Third, we shall examine reliability and robustness questions for the various models, with random structures and probabilistic analysis playing a crucial role. Inseparable from the work on models is the development of new algorithms to exploit the power of certain models, and the exploration of the relationships between different models. We shall develop basic algorithms that support efficient use of massively parallel computers, e.g., synchronisation protocols, virtual shared-memory models, mapping of a process graph onto a given parallel architecture, routing and load balancing, and central communication tasks like broadcasting or gossiping. The results achieved within this area will influence the development of parallel operating systems as described in Section 1.3. Our algorithms will be designed and analysed both for the standard PRAM model and the more detailed models mentioned above. Performance and analysis will be experimentally tested using FRAMES. The algorithms will be made available by developing an experimental library of basic PRAM algorithms and data structures, called PADPAD (for PRAM Algorithms and Data structures), using a language environment designed by research groups at the universities of Saarbrücken and Trier. An important target architecture will be the PRAM being constructed by W. Paul's group at Saarbrücken.
