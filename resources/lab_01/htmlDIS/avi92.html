Proceedings (Published by World Scientific: Advanced Visual Interfaces - World Scientific Series in Computer Science, Vol.36, 1993.)

The Proceedings volume contains the papers presented at the Advanced Visual Interfaces Workshop, AVI '92 held in Rome on May 27-29, 1992 at the Congress Center of the University of Rome "La Sapienza". The aim of this Workshop was to bring together experts in different disciplines having a common interest in the design and management of visual interfaces. We believe that such interfaces, exploiting the human ability to perceive and detect visual signs like forms, diagrams, icons, images, etc., are able to improve human-computer interaction for a variety of tasks. Typical applications in which these interfaces could be particularly useful are: accessing databases, monitoring real time complex systems, modeling concurrent processes, etc.

Only recently, software designers have started to reduce the semantic gap between the computer and the user: in fact the communication level must be raised by designing new human-oriented interfaces that try to understand the user behaviour rather than impose a machine-oriented behaviour. The user is gradually coming to the forefront, cooperating in the interface developmentfrom the design stage onwards, as widely agreed during the Workshop. The design of a good interface is the crucial point for a better and wider use of computers. Progress in this field requires contributions of researchers coming from different areas, e.g. software engineering, ergonomics, semiotics, linguistics, graphics, etc., as proved by the differences in background and expertise of the workshop participants.

We had nearly one hundred attendees coming from several countries, both from industry and academia, discussing theoretical as well as applicative aspects of visual interfaces. Five invited speakers, namely Shi-Kuo Chang (University of Pittsburgh, USA), Steve Feiner (Columbia University, USA), Jock Mackinlay (Xerox PARC, USA), Joy Mountford (Apple, USA), and Wolfgang Wahlster (University of Saarbrücken, Germany) shared with the audience their basic contributions to the Workshop areas. The papers contained in the Proceedings, about half of those submitted to the Workshop, have been grouped, depending on their content, into seven chapter, entitled: Visual Interface Design, Visual Interaction, Visualization Methods, User Models, Formal Methods, Graphical Layout Strategies, and a last chapter containing four short papers describing system prototypes.

The Workshop has been made possible through the generous contributions of a number of sponsoring institutions. We are strongly indebted to the members of the Program Committee who unsparingly used their precious time to help us to set up the scientific program of the Workshop; thanks are also given to the referees who carefully and promptly reviewed all the submitted papers. Finally, special thanks are due to Giuseppe Santucci for his precious assistance and his coordination of a special session presenting system demonstrations.
