The 9th International Conference on Database Theory
Invited talks

Siena, Italy, 8-10 January 2003


 

Bioinformatics Adventures in Database Research,
by Limsoon Wong

Informatics has helped launch molecular biology into the genomic era. It appears certain that informatics will continue to be a major contributor to the success of molecular biology in the post-genome era. In this paper, we discuss our experience of data integration and datamining in bioinformatics, as well as the role that database theory played in these topics. We also describe LIMS as a third key topic in bioinformatics where advances in database system and theory can be very relevant. (Joint work with Jinyan Lee and See-Kiong Ng)
 

Approximations in Database Systems,
by Yannis Ioannidis

The need for approximations of information has become very critical in the recent past. From traditional query optimization to newer functionality like user feedback and knowledge discovery, data management systems require quick delivery of approximate data in order to serve their goals. There are several techniques that have been proposed to solve the problem, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. In this talk, we will take a look at some of the most important data approximation problems and put them side by side with some of the most prominent techniques that have been proposed to solve them. We will then hint on some open problems that appear whose solution would have some great impact in the area.
 

Open Problems in Peer-to-Peer Systems,
by Hector Garcia-Molina

In a Peer-To-Peer (P2P) system, autonomous computers at the "edge" of the network pool their resources, e.g., files, storage, compute cycles, in order to inexpensively handle tasks that would normally require large costly servers. Current P2P systems include Morpheus, Gnutella, Seti@Home, and have hundreds of thousands of users. The scale of these systems, their "open nature" and the lack of centralized control, pose tough performance and security challenges. In this talk I will present a "taxonomy" of P2P research areas, including search, resource management, and security. In addition, I will discuss particular open problems, and briefly summarize some ongoing research efforts at Stanford.
