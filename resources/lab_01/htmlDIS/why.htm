Why are they so special?

Positive systems have a long and rich history with antecedents in the work of Markov, Perron and Frobenius, Leontieff and Leslie, just to mention a few, but the unifying approach of system theory was initiated by David Luenberger. In fact, as explained in his textbook on dynamical systems, he firmly believed that:

The theory of positive systems is deep and elegant -- and yet pleasantly consistent with intuition. [...] Indeed, just the knowledge that the system is positive allows one to make some fairly strong statements about its behaviour; these statements being true no matter what values the parameters may happen to take. It is for positive systems, therefore, that dynamic systems theory assumes one of its most potent forms.

His intuition was so convincing that, from that time on, an impressive  number of theoretical contributions and applications to this field has appeared  in the literature. In fact, the modern approach to mathematical modeling is  based on two fundamental concepts: multidisciplinarity and model robustness.

As a matter of fact, positivity is a property arising in many disciplines and David Luenberger envisaged model robustness in his statement regarding positive systems reported above.

To illustrate this point, it must be observed that, as well explained by Morris Hirsch1, the most spectacular use of mathematics in science has been to predict accurately and successfully observations regarding the Nature, such as the motion of planets or the atomic structure. But, much of science concerns not only predictions, but also understanding: How galaxies form? How did species arises? How do economies develop? Dynamical systems theory plays an important role in trying to answer such questions, but its role is rather different from its more familiar one as an exclusively predictive tool. Especially, as we move away from physics toward the multidisciplinary world of the biological and economical sciences or in general to the so-called complex systems science, we find that dynamics is used more as a source of “qualitative” insight than for making precise quantitative predictions. Moreover, also the modern concept of prediction is more flexible, as for the case of “scenario predictions”.
 

In fact, mathematical models in biological and economical sciences are conceptual models that may shine a clear light on certain tendencies or relationships – not necessarily ``laws" – that would otherwise remain obscure. More importantly, for such conclusions to be convincing, the mathematical deductions from the model must be robust – i.e. insensitive to mathematical detail. This somewhat vague but fundamental concept refers to the persistence of some specified features of the mathematical system even in the presence of perturbations. The magnitude and the nature of perturbations may  vary in different context, but the concept remains the same: the underlying idea - that mathematical models should be robust in their important feature - is widely accepted. In fact, in every mathematical investigations the question will arise on whether or not the results can be applied to the real world.
